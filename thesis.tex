\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Chest X-ray imaging is one of the most widely used diagnostic tools for detecting thoracic abnormalities, yet manual interpretation can be time-consuming and subject to inter-observer variability. Deep learning has shown strong potential for assisting radiographic diagnosis, but model performance often depends on the availability of labeled data and model decisions are frequently difficult to interpret. This thesis investigates whether transfer learning and explainable deep learning can improve chest X-ray diagnostic classification in a focused and reproducible experimental setting. The study uses the NIH ChestXray14 dataset and formulates the task as binary classification, where images labeled ``No Finding'' are treated as normal and images with any pathology label are treated as abnormal. Two convolutional neural network architectures, ResNet50 and DenseNet121, are evaluated under two training settings: training from scratch and transfer learning from ImageNet-pretrained weights. Model performance is assessed using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, area under the ROC curve, and confusion-matrix-based error analysis. To improve interpretability, Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) is applied to representative predictions in order to visualize the image regions that most strongly influence classification decisions. The expected contribution of this thesis is a clear comparative analysis of model architecture and training strategy for binary chest X-ray classification, together with an interpretable evaluation of model behavior. This work aims to provide a practical and academically grounded capstone study on the role of transfer learning and explainability in medical image analysis.

\textbf{Keywords:} chest X-ray classification; transfer learning; deep learning; Grad-CAM; medical image analysis.
