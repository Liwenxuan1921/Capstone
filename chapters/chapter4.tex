\chapter{Experiments and Results}

\section{Experimental Setup}
This chapter presents the experimental results for the four core model configurations defined in Chapter 3. The goal is to compare architecture choice and training strategy under a common evaluation setting. All experiments use the same binary classification task, the same preprocessing pipeline, and the same train-validation-test split. This ensures that any observed performance differences can be interpreted mainly in terms of the model architecture or the use of transfer learning rather than changes in data preparation.

The four experiments evaluated in this thesis are listed below:
\begin{itemize}
    \item E1: ResNet50 trained from scratch;
    \item E2: ResNet50 with transfer learning;
    \item E3: DenseNet121 trained from scratch;
    \item E4: DenseNet121 with transfer learning.
\end{itemize}

The experiments reported in this chapter use the finalized NIH ChestXray14 binary split introduced in Chapter 3, with 78,202 training images, 17,099 validation images, and 16,819 test images. All four experiments were trained with an image size of 224, batch size of 16, Adam optimizer, learning rate of 0.001, weight decay of 0.0001, and a maximum of 20 epochs, with early stopping applied when no validation improvement was observed for five consecutive epochs. The same loss function, preprocessing pipeline, and evaluation metrics were retained across runs to keep the comparison controlled and interpretable.

\section{Baseline Results}
This section reports the results of the two scratch-training experiments. These baselines are important because they establish how well each model performs without relying on pretrained visual features. They also provide a direct reference point for evaluating the later transfer-learning experiments.

The completed scratch runs show that DenseNet121 trained from scratch slightly outperformed ResNet50 trained from scratch across all major test metrics, including accuracy, recall, F1-score, and AUC. This is an important result because it demonstrates that pretrained weights were not uniformly necessary for strong performance under the binary task formulation used in this thesis.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Baseline results for models trained from scratch}
    \label{tab:baseline-results}
    \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
        \toprule
        Model & Accuracy & Precision & Recall & F1-score & AUC \\
        \midrule
        ResNet50 & 0.6732 & 0.6748 & 0.5596 & 0.6118 & 0.7225 \\
        DenseNet121 & 0.6912 & 0.6913 & 0.5945 & 0.6393 & 0.7457 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Among the two scratch baselines, DenseNet121 delivered the stronger result. It achieved a test AUC of 0.7457 compared with 0.7225 for ResNet50, while also producing higher accuracy and F1-score. This suggests that DenseNet121 was able to learn a more effective feature representation from the dataset even without external pretraining. At the same time, both scratch baselines remained clinically imperfect, particularly in recall, which means that a substantial fraction of abnormal cases would still be missed under the current threshold.

\section{Transfer Learning Results}
This section presents the results obtained when ResNet50 and DenseNet121 were initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights. The purpose is to determine whether transfer learning improves performance relative to scratch training and whether the gains are consistent across both architectures.

Transfer learning was evaluated using the same test metrics for both architectures. In the completed experiments, both pretrained models achieved test AUC values above 0.74, showing that pretrained visual representations transferred effectively to this binary chest X-ray task. However, the two models produced noticeably different error profiles. ResNet50 delivered the strongest overall balance across accuracy, recall, F1-score, and AUC, whereas DenseNet121 achieved higher precision and specificity at the cost of substantially lower recall.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Results for models trained with transfer learning}
    \label{tab:transfer-results}
    \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{%
    \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
        \toprule
        Model & Accuracy & Precision & Recall & F1-score & AUC \\
        \midrule
        ResNet50 + transfer learning & 0.6955 & 0.6847 & 0.6272 & 0.6547 & 0.7454 \\
        DenseNet121 + transfer learning & 0.6711 & 0.7225 & 0.4634 & 0.5646 & 0.7421 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}%
    }
\end{table}

The ResNet50 transfer-learning run completed all 20 epochs and achieved its best validation AUC at epoch 20. On the test set, it reached an AUC of 0.7454, accuracy of 0.6955, and F1-score of 0.6547. These values indicate that the model maintained a relatively balanced tradeoff between sensitivity and specificity. The DenseNet121 transfer-learning run was resumed on a second machine from the checkpoint saved after epoch 7 and stopped early at epoch 17. Its best validation AUC was 0.7348 at epoch 12, and its final test AUC was 0.7421. Although the AUC difference between the two models is small, the DenseNet121 model produced a much lower recall of 0.4634, which indicates that more abnormal cases were missed under the current threshold.

At the same time, DenseNet121 achieved higher precision (0.7225) and specificity (0.8482) than ResNet50, suggesting that it was more conservative in assigning the abnormal label. This tradeoff is important in a medical screening context. A higher-recall model reduces the risk of missed abnormal cases, whereas a higher-specificity model reduces the number of false alarms. Under the binary formulation used in this study, ResNet50 appears to be the better balanced model, while DenseNet121 may be preferred only when the reduction of false positives is the dominant requirement.

\section{Comparative Analysis}
After presenting baseline and transfer-learning results separately, all four experiments can now be compared in one integrated analysis. This section is the core of the chapter because it connects the numerical results back to the thesis research questions. The goal is not only to identify the strongest model, but also to explain the tradeoffs between performance, efficiency, and interpretability.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Final comparison of all four experiments}
    \label{tab:final-comparison}
    \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{%
    \begin{tabular}{lccccccc}
        \toprule
        Experiment & Accuracy & Precision & Recall & F1-score & AUC & Sensitivity & Specificity \\
        \midrule
        E1: ResNet50 scratch & 0.6732 & 0.6748 & 0.5596 & 0.6118 & 0.7225 & 0.5596 & 0.7700 \\
        E2: ResNet50 transfer & 0.6955 & 0.6847 & 0.6272 & 0.6547 & 0.7454 & 0.6272 & 0.7537 \\
        E3: DenseNet121 scratch & 0.6912 & 0.6913 & 0.5945 & 0.6393 & 0.7457 & 0.5945 & 0.7736 \\
        E4: DenseNet121 transfer & 0.6711 & 0.7225 & 0.4634 & 0.5646 & 0.7421 & 0.4634 & 0.8482 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}%
    }
\end{table}

The four-way comparison reveals two important patterns. First, the best result depends on the evaluation priority. DenseNet121 trained from scratch achieved the highest overall test AUC at 0.7457, narrowly exceeding ResNet50 with transfer learning at 0.7454. However, ResNet50 with transfer learning produced the highest accuracy, recall, and F1-score, which made it the most balanced model for the screening-oriented objective of detecting abnormal cases while avoiding an excessive precision-recall tradeoff.

Second, transfer learning did not affect the two architectures in the same way. For ResNet50, transfer learning improved every major headline metric relative to the scratch baseline, raising test accuracy from 0.6732 to 0.6955, recall from 0.5596 to 0.6272, F1-score from 0.6118 to 0.6547, and AUC from 0.7225 to 0.7454. For DenseNet121, the opposite pattern appeared: the scratch model slightly outperformed the transfer-learning model in accuracy, recall, F1-score, and AUC, while the transfer-learning version achieved notably higher precision and specificity. This suggests that ImageNet pretraining made DenseNet121 more conservative under the current threshold rather than uniformly stronger.

Taken together, the final results suggest that transfer learning is clearly beneficial for ResNet50, but not uniformly beneficial for DenseNet121 in this binary formulation. If the main goal is a balanced screening-oriented classifier, ResNet50 with transfer learning remains the most defensible choice because of its stronger recall and F1-score. If AUC alone is prioritized, DenseNet121 trained from scratch is marginally strongest. These tradeoffs justify the later discussion of threshold sensitivity, task formulation, and explainability.

\section{Grad-CAM Visualization Results}

Four representative test cases were selected to cover correct normal prediction, correct abnormal prediction, false positive, and false negative. This set was chosen so that the visualization analysis would reflect both model strengths and model failure modes.

In the correct normal case shown in Figure \ref{fig:gradcam-correct-normal}, the model focuses mainly on the central thoracic region rather than on borders or obvious acquisition artifacts. This suggests that the classifier is relying on broad chest structure when making a normal decision. In the correct abnormal case shown in Figure \ref{fig:gradcam-correct-abnormal}, the strongest activation is located in clinically relevant thoracic areas, with more emphasis placed on the right lung field. This behavior is consistent with the expectation that abnormal predictions should be driven by localized image evidence inside the chest region.

The error cases reveal a more mixed picture. In the false positive example in Figure \ref{fig:gradcam-false-positive}, the heatmap is relatively diffuse and weighted toward one hemithorax, which suggests that the model may sometimes overreact to intensity variation, positioning, or imaging artifacts in otherwise normal radiographs. In the false negative example in Figure \ref{fig:gradcam-false-negative}, the activation remains narrow and centrally concentrated, indicating that the model may fail to respond strongly enough when abnormal findings are subtle or do not dominate the overall appearance of the chest X-ray.

Overall, the Grad-CAM results provide partial support for the interpretability of the best-performing model. The model generally attends to anatomically plausible thoracic regions rather than to entirely irrelevant background areas, which is encouraging. At the same time, the error cases show that plausible attention maps do not guarantee correct prediction, and Grad-CAM should therefore be interpreted as supportive evidence rather than definitive clinical explanation.
